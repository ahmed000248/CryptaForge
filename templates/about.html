Cryptographic Algorithms Study Guide

A theoretical reference manual covering history, mathematical operations, and security evaluations of classical ciphers.

Caesar Cipher

Security Level: Very Weak
History

Named after Julius Caesar, who used it with a shift of 3 to protect military correspondences. It is one of the oldest recorded substitution ciphers in history (around 100 BC).

Working Principle

Each character in the plaintext is shifted forward by a fixed key number positions in the alphabet. If the shift goes beyond 'Z', it wraps around to the beginning of the alphabet.

Mathematical Definition:
E(x) = (x + k) mod 26
D(x) = (x - k) mod 26
Practical Example

Plaintext: HELLO, Key: 3
H (7) + 3 = 10 (K)
E (4) + 3 = 7 (H)
L (11) + 3 = 14 (O)
L (11) + 3 = 14 (O)
O (14) + 3 = 17 (R)
Ciphertext: KHOOR

Playfair Cipher

Security Level: Weak
History

Invented by Charles Wheatstone in 1854, but named after Lord Playfair who promoted its use. It was the first practical digraph substitution cipher and was used extensively for tactical communication by British forces in World War I.

Working Principle

Uses a 5x5 matrix containing a keyword (with duplicates removed and letters 'I' and 'J' sharing a cell). Plaintext is broken into pairs of letters (digraphs). Double letters are separated by inserting 'X'. Transformations are applied: same row shifts right circular; same column shifts down circular; rectangle corners swap columns.

Practical Example

Key: MONARCHY (Matrix fills: M-O-N-A-R / C-H-Y-B-D / E-F-G-I-K / L-P-Q-S-T / U-V-W-X-Z)
Plaintext: HELLO -> digraphs: HE, LX, LO
For HE (rectangle corners): H and E map to EC
Ciphertext: ECQZTB

Hill Cipher

Security Level: Moderate
History

Invented by Lester S. Hill in 1929. It was the first polygraphic cipher that could practical operate on more than three letters at once, using linear matrix algebra.

Working Principle

Text is processed in blocks of size n (2 for 2x2). Letters are treated as numbers (A=0, B=1...). A vector of character values is multiplied by an n x n key matrix. The results are calculated modulo 26 to produce ciphertext. Decryption requires multiplying by the modular inverse of the key matrix.

Practical Example

Key Matrix: [[7, 8], [11, 11]], Plaintext block: HI [7, 8]
C1 = (7*7 + 8*8) % 26 = (49 + 64) % 26 = 113 % 26 = 9 (J)
C2 = (11*7 + 11*8) % 26 = (77 + 88) % 26 = 165 % 26 = 9 (J)
Result: JJ

Columnar Transposition Cipher

Security Level: Moderate
History

Used extensively in historical military communication, including by the Union Army in the American Civil War, and both sides during the World Wars. It is a transposition-type cipher rather than a substitution-type.

Working Principle

The plaintext is written horizontally into a grid of width equal to the key keyword length. The columns are then read out vertically one by one, according to the alphabetical order of the characters in the keyword.

Practical Example

Key: CAR (Order: A=2, C=1, R=3). Text: HELLOWORLD (padded to 12: HELLOWORLDXX)
Grid:
H E L
L O W
O R L
D X X
Read Col 2 (E,O,R,X), then Col 1 (H,L,O,D), then Col 3 (L,W,L,X)
Ciphertext: EORXHLODLWLX

Rail Fence Cipher

Security Level: Weak
History

One of the simplest transposition ciphers. Ancient Greeks used a physical tool called a 'scytale' which operated on similar mechanical principles of wrapping text around cylindrical rods of different sizes.

Working Principle

Plaintext is written downwards diagonally on successive 'rails' of a fence, then moving upwards and repeating, forming a zig-zag waves pattern. The ciphertext is then constructed by reading the rails horizontally row-by-row.

Practical Example

Plaintext: SECRET, Rails: 3
S . . . E .
. E . R . T
. . C . . .
Read Row 0 (SE), Row 1 (ERT), Row 2 (C)
Ciphertext: SEERTC

Custom Substitution Cipher

Security Level: Custom
History

Custom substitution ciphers represent a generalized form of monoalphabetic substitution, where any of the 26! (4.03 x 10^26) possible alphabets is used instead of a simple shift (like Caesar).

Working Principle

Each letter in the standard alphabet is mapped directly to a user-defined custom letter sequence of 26 letters. When encrypting, each letter in the plaintext is replaced by the corresponding custom letter at the same index.

Practical Example

Normal alphabet: ABCDEFGHIJKLMNOPQRSTUVWXYZ
Custom alphabet: QWERTYUIOPASDFGHJKLZXCVBNM
Plaintext: CAB
C -> E, A -> Q, B -> W
Ciphertext: EQW
